01

Overview & Research Context

This dashboard maps 13 major announced data center deals and approximately 35 established operational campuses across Virginia and Maryland. It overlays utility service territories, PJM transmission infrastructure, and five analytical panels to support research on the grid, fiscal, and governance implications of the data center boom.

The central policy question the dashboard is designed to illuminate: Who pays for AI's power appetite, and under what regulatory arrangements? Virginia hosts roughly 35% of global data center capacity — earning the designation "first AI resource state" — while simultaneously navigating the contested NextEra–Dominion Energy merger, a $1.9B/yr sales tax exemption under political pressure, and a PJM transmission grid that was not designed for gigawatt-scale point loads.

The word Netzlast is German for "grid load" — a nod to the German engineering tradition of treating electrical infrastructure as a public system to be planned, not simply a market to be cleared.

Research affiliation

This dashboard supports the research project "Who Pays for AI's Power Appetite? Virginia as the First AI Resource State" — School of Public and International Affairs, Virginia Tech. It is not affiliated with or endorsed by any utility, data center operator, or regulatory body.

Reading Deal Markers

Each announced deal is represented by a composite marker encoding four independent variables simultaneously. Understanding the encoding unlocks the map's full analytical value.

Outer ring = utility (color + dash)
Circle fill = deal status
Upper-right dot = state (VA blue, MD green)
Lower-left dot = project type
Circle size = investment ($B, √-scaled)

Status colors

  • ■ Active — Under development with confirmed commitments or active construction.
  • ■ Dead / halted — Project withdrawn, rejected, or permanently cancelled (e.g. PW Digital Gateway).
  • ■ Contested / paused — Has approvals or committed tenants but faces a moratorium, legal challenge, or significant community opposition.
  • ■ Announced — Publicly announced but no construction or binding commitments confirmed.

Utility ring styles

  • Dominion Energy — Solid blue ring. Merger-exposed (NextEra acquisition pending).
  • BGE / Exelon — Solid purple ring.
  • Pepco / Exelon — Dashed brown ring.
  • Rappahannock EC (REC) — Dotted forest-green ring. Cooperative; not Dominion-exposed.
  • Appalachian Power (AEP) — Short-dashed red ring. Serves Berry Hill (Southside VA).
Reading tip

Hover over any marker for a summary tooltip. Click to open the full detail card, which shows all 12 data fields including cooling type, PUE, grid interconnection status, opposition level, and tax exemption status.

Layer Toggles

Five toggleable layers control what is drawn on the map. Each button in the toolbar is highlighted when its layer is on; click to toggle. Layers can be combined freely.

▦ Utility areas County-level utility service territories. Dominion (blue), BGE (purple), Pepco (orange-brown), REC (green), AEP (rose), NOVEC, and Maryland co-ops shown at ~62% opacity. Note that boundaries are approximate county-level assignments; cooperative territories are especially irregular at sub-county scales.
⬡ Density NoVA 300+ data center cluster zones. Four amber halo-circles centered on the world's densest concentration of data centers. Circle area is scaled to geographic footprint. Short count labels (e.g. "~4,000 MW · 300+ facilities") appear above each zone. Hover for a full facility-count summary per sub-cluster.
◇ Established ~35 operational campuses shown as small gray diamonds. A curated sample only — Northern Virginia alone has 600+ total operational data centers. Hover for operator name and approximate capacity in MW.
⚡ Grid lines PJM transmission infrastructure — 500kV and 230kV lines, coded by status. Named substation squares appear at key nodes. Hover any line segment for voltage, status, and the relevant SCC or regulatory case number. See Section 8 for full details.
▨ Reg. zones Moratorium & contested zoning overlays. Three counties shaded when active — Prince George's Co. MD and Montgomery Co. MD (red hatching, active data center moratoria) and Prince William Co. VA (amber, rezoning voided by VA Court of Appeals). Hover for a tooltip describing the specific regulatory status and deal affected.
◉ Risk map Regulatory risk choropleth. Colors each county that contains a deal by the aggregate flashpoint score of the highest-risk deal within it (mint green = low risk, rose = high risk, 0–18 scale). Off by default. Useful overlay when the utility layer is turned off — the two encode complementary information (who serves the county vs. how contested development is). See Section 9 for the full scoring methodology.
◆ Gen. plants 15 major power stations sized by capacity (MW, √-scaled) and coloured by fuel type. Circle letter: N = Nuclear, G = Gas, C = Coal, P = Pumped-storage, H = Hydro, W = Offshore wind. Dashed ring = under construction; dashed outline = retired. Hover for full tooltip including capacity, owner, status, and policy significance. Off by default — toggle on to overlay generation supply alongside transmission and demand.
Performance note

At very high zoom levels (10×–14×), toggling all layers simultaneously can be slow on older devices. The grid lines layer is the most computationally intensive due to substation label rendering.

07

Project Typology

Each deal is classified into one of five mutually exclusive types. The typology is encoded as the lower-left dot on each map marker, the stacked fill in Panel A, and the scatter color in Panel C.

Hyperscale (own-operate)
A major cloud provider (AWS, Google, Microsoft) that builds and operates data centers for its own workloads. Characterized by very high investment totals, multi-decade timelines, and direct utility relationships. Tends toward higher $/MW than wholesale builds.
AWS Northern Virginia, Google Chesterfield
AI / GPU cluster
Explicitly positioned for AI training and inference workloads requiring very high rack power density (30–100+ kW/rack vs. 5–10 kW legacy). Often wholesale-leased to a hyperscale AI tenant, but distinguished by facility design, cooling architecture, and the scale of the power contingency.
STACK / Berry Hill, STACK / Stafford Campus
Wholesale developer
A developer builds a campus to spec for one or more anchor tenants under long-term wholesale leases. The developer owns the physical infrastructure; the tenant brings the compute load. The most common model in the dataset.
CleanArc VA1, EdgeCore Louisa, PowerHouse 95, Vantage VA4
Multi-developer campus
A large multi-building campus developed by a consortium or partnership, with multiple operators and tenants. More complex governance, shared infrastructure, and typically contested permitting given scale. Partnership disputes are common (see Quantum Frederick).
Quantum Frederick, Brightseat Tech Park
Speculative / no committed tenant
Site has been announced or zoned for data center use but has no confirmed anchor tenant, construction start date, or binding power commitment as of May 2026. Capacity estimates are therefore highly uncertain (marked with †).
Woodlawn / Baltimore Co., Dickerson / Montgomery Co.

MPRP context

The Maryland Piedmont Reliability Project is a ~70-mile, 500kV line contracted by PJM and assigned to PSEG, running from the Doubs substation in southern Frederick County, MD through Carroll County to northern Baltimore County. It faces opposition from all three counties, the Maryland Farm Bureau, and a Stop MPRP Inc. advocacy coalition. MD PSC procedural hearings run through 2027; projected operation June 2027 if approved. It is primarily driven by data center load growth at Quantum Frederick and future Maryland sites.

Limitations & Caveats

  • Utility assignments are county-level approximations. Cooperative boundaries (REC, NOVEC, SMECO) are especially irregular at sub-county scales. A deal labeled "Dominion" may be within a few miles of a cooperative boundary.
  • Established campus inventory is a curated sample. Northern Virginia alone has 600+ operational data centers. The ~35 facilities shown are selected for representative coverage of operators and geography, not completeness.
  • Capacity estimates (†) are indicative. For GW-scale projects with undisclosed final capacity (Berry Hill, AWS multi-GW, Quantum Frederick), mwNum reflects conservative planning estimates derived from public statements and site footprint. They should not be quoted as stated capacity figures.
  • Investment figures mix stages. Some investments are 30-year totals; others are initial phase commitments. The inv field attempts to capture total announced investment where possible, but comparability across deals is limited.
  • PUE values are sparse. Only AWS and Google disclose fleet-level PUE. Panel E benchmarks for other types are industry estimates from facility design literature, not measured values.
  • Transmission line coordinates are approximate. The grid overlay is drawn from SCC case descriptions, county planning documents, and PSEG MPRP routing materials — not from surveyed GIS data.
  • Status reflects May 2026. This is a fast-moving landscape. The PW Digital Gateway legal outcome, MPRP regulatory timeline, and VA DCSA exemption reform are all in flux. No automated update mechanism exists; the dataset requires manual revision.
  • The dashboard covers announced deals only. A substantial volume of data center activity occurs through quiet land acquisition, incremental expansions of existing facilities, and undisclosed hyperscale expansions that never appear in press releases.

Sources & Methodology

Deal data was assembled from a combination of local news, trade press, regulatory filings, and company press releases. All data reflects public information available as of May 2026. No non-public information from utility filings, developer documents, or government sources was used.

  • Local newsWSLS (Roanoke), Virginia Business, Virginia Mercury, Baltimore Banner, Maryland Matters, The Daily Record (Baltimore)
  • Trade pressData Center Dynamics, Bisnow, Commercial Observer, Data Center Frontier, Inside Climate News
  • Economic developmentVirginia Economic Development Partnership (VEDP); Maryland Commerce; JLARC 2024 data center report; VEDP Biennial DCRSUT Report (Jan 2026)
  • Regulatory filingsVirginia SCC transmission project register; Maryland PSC Case 9773 (MPRP); Prince George's County planning board records; VA Court of Appeals PW Digital Gateway decision
  • Company disclosuresEdgeCore press release (Jun 2025); STACK Infrastructure Stafford announcement (Jan 2025); Lerner Enterprises / Brightseat planning submissions; TPG Real Estate / Quantum Frederick filings
  • Grid / transmissionDominion Energy 2024 IRP; PJM load forecast 2025; PSEG MPRP routing documents; SCC transmission project register; Dominion Grid Transformation Plan
  • Legal / courtCompass Datacenters withdrawal (Apr 2026); SEC filings; Prince George's County moratorium executive order (Sep 2025); Quantum Loophole court proceedings (Sep 2024)
  • PUE / efficiencyAWS sustainability disclosure 2023 (fleet PUE 1.15); Google environmental report (PUE 1.12); STACK Infrastructure cooling white paper; industry benchmarks from Data Center Dynamics and Uptime Institute

Methodology notes

  • County FIPS → utility assignments were built from VEDP service territory maps, EIA Form 861 data, and cross-referenced against individual deal announcements where discrepancies existed (notably Berry Hill → AEP, not Dominion).
  • Investment figures for dead projects (PW Digital Gateway) reflect the investment that would have been made if built, as stated in planning documents, not actual expenditure.
  • The Berry Hill ($100B) figure is the 30-year total economic impact as stated by Pittsylvania County officials and STACK Infrastructure; the initial capital commitment tied to the AEP 299 MW contingency is substantially lower.
  • Opposition scores (none / low / petition / legal / moratorium) are assessed against the highest level of organized formal opposition observed as of May 2026, not current trajectory.

Policy context: generation vs. demand

Total VA+MD operating generation (19,857 MW) exceeds total active announced data center demand (12,770 MW) on paper. But this aggregate comparison is misleading: generation capacity is not fully dispatchable, much of it is already committed to residential and commercial loads, and data center demand is concentrated in a small geographic area (NoVA / Ashburn) while generation is dispersed. The more meaningful figure is that only 58% of announced data center demand has a signed interconnection agreement.

Nuclear plants (40% of VA+MD generation, 8,096 MW) run as baseload and cannot be dispatched to serve incremental demand. Bath County Pumped Storage (3,003 MW) charges from existing nuclear output overnight and discharges at peak — but the near-flat 24h demand profile of large data centers fundamentally alters this dispatch model. See Panel G for the full supply-demand vulnerability analysis.
